**Step 1 – Analyze Editing Instructions and Determine Allowed Changes**

**Description of reference image:**  
The subject is a large, ornate leather-bound book standing upright on a dark wooden desk, its cover featuring an elaborate oval-shaped embossed frame with intricate floral and scrollwork patterns in aged gold-brown tones against a black textured leather background. The spine displays raised bands and gold-embossed text (though illegible), suggesting antiquity. To the right of the book stands a golden quill holder containing a single light brown feather quill, positioned vertically. In front of the book lies a small glass inkwell with dark ink, resting on a scalloped brass tray. Nearby, a folded parchment paper with faint cursive script and a silver spoon-like object rest on the surface. Behind the main book are several stacked books with similar vintage bindings, and to the left, a stack of worn, dark-toned volumes. A black dome-shaped desk lamp hangs overhead, casting warm directional light onto the scene, creating soft shadows and highlighting textures. The background is a muted dark gray wall, contributing to a moody, scholarly atmosphere.

---

**Thinking process:**  
The editing instruction specifies transforming the **ornate book**, **quill**, and **inkwell** into a **detailed 3D miniature diorama housed within a glass cloche**. This indicates that the **form and physical presence** of these three objects can be radically altered—replaced by their miniature versions inside a protective dome. However, the instruction also describes **how they should be reimagined**:  
- The **book** becomes an open pop-up with **miniature carved figures** — so its function as a book remains, but its form changes.  
- The **quill** becomes a **central elegant pivot** — implying it retains its identity as a quill but is repurposed spatially.  
- The **inkwell** becomes a **glistening fountain** — again, transformed but conceptually linked.  
- The **base** is now a **wooden disk etched with a map of imagined lands**, adding a new thematic layer.

Crucially, the instruction **does not mention** retaining any elements of the original environment such as:  
- The **desk**, **stacks of books**, **lamp**, or **parchment papers**  
- The **lighting setup** or **background wall**  

Thus, the **scene environment** is **not required to be preserved** and may be changed or removed entirely.

However, the instruction **explicitly references** the **book**, **quill**, and **inkwell** as subjects to be transformed — meaning their **core identities** must be retained in some form. Even though their forms change, they must still be recognizable as those items.

Additionally, the **glass cloche** and **miniature diorama** are introduced as new structures, indicating that the **original spatial layout** and **object relationships** (e.g., book beside quill) are being replaced.

Therefore, the **allowed changes** include:
- The **physical form** of the book, quill, and inkwell
- The **environmental setting** (desk, stacks, lamp, etc.)
- The **spatial arrangement** of objects
- The **material properties** of the objects (leather → pop-up, metal → fountain)
- The **presence of secondary items** like parchment and spoon

But the **identity** of the core trio — book, quill, inkwell — must remain intact through transformation.

---

**Allowed Changes List:**  
- The form and material of the ornate book (can become pop-up structure)  
- The shape and placement of the quill (can become central pivot)  
- The design of the inkwell (can become glistening fountain)  
- The surrounding environment (desk, stacks, lamp, parchment, spoon, etc.)  
- The lighting and background  
- The spatial configuration of objects  
- The texture and color of the objects (within reason, as long as identity is preserved)  
- The addition of the glass cloche and wooden base with map

**Step 2 – Determine Elements That Should Remain Unchanged**

**Thinking process:**  
We now determine what **must remain unchanged** despite transformation, based on:

1. **Core recognition elements**: These are visual features essential for identifying the object as *what it is*. For example:
   - The **book** must still be identifiable as a book — even if it's a pop-up, it must have pages, binding, and resemble a book.
   - The **quill** must retain its feathered form — even if it’s a pivot, it must look like a quill.
   - The **inkwell** must be recognizable as a container for ink — even if it’s a fountain, it must suggest holding liquid.

2. **Functional/logical association elements**: 
   - The **book**, **quill**, and **inkwell** are traditionally used together in writing. Their functional relationship must be preserved in the diorama — i.e., they should still appear connected by purpose (writing tools).
   - If the quill is a "pivot", it must still imply movement or use in writing, not just decoration.

3. **Non-modified element deduction**:
   - Since the instruction does **not** mention preserving the **stacked books**, **lamp**, **parchment**, or **silver spoon**, these can be omitted or altered.
   - However, the **original visual identity** of the **three key objects** must be preserved in their transformed state — because otherwise, the edit loses coherence with the original.

Thus, the **preservation targets** are not about literal replication, but about **retaining the core visual identity** of each object under transformation.

Specifically:
- The **book** must retain its **rectangular shape**, **binding**, and **page structure** — even if pop-up.
- The **quill** must retain its **feathered shaft** and **tapered tip** — even if it’s a pivot.
- The **inkwell** must retain its **container shape** and **liquid content** — even if it’s a fountain.

These are the **minimum visual anchors** needed to ensure the edited image remains faithful to the original subject.

---

**Preservation Targets List:**  
- The ornate book must be recognizable as a book (with binding, pages, rectangular form)  
- The quill must retain its feathered form and taper  
- The inkwell must retain its role as a container for ink  
- The functional triad (book, quill, inkwell) must remain associated as writing tools  
- The overall theme of antiquity and scholarship must be preserved (via style, materials, colors)  

(Note: While the environment changes, the **thematic essence** — vintage, scholarly, literary — is implied by the instruction’s “vintage explorer theme” and thus should be maintained in tone.)
