**Step 1 – Analyze Editing Instructions and Determine Allowed Changes**

**Description of reference image:**  
The subject consists of three figurines representing a nativity scene: Mary kneeling on the left, Joseph standing on the right leaning against a rock, and the infant Jesus lying in the center on a white fluffy surface. Mary wears a pale pink robe with a gray-blue shawl draped over her shoulders and head, a white veil covering her hair, and a golden halo above her head. Her hands are clasped in prayer. Joseph is dressed in layered robes—light brown undergarment with a darker brown outer cloak—and has a beard, long hair, and a golden halo; he holds a staff topped with a white lily flower. The infant Jesus lies on his back, bare except for a small white diaper, with golden wing-like rays behind his head symbolizing divinity. All figures rest on rocky bases resembling natural stone. The background is plain white, emphasizing the figurines.

Thinking process:  
The editing instruction explicitly states to “transform the nativity scene figures into an intricate Renaissance fresco,” indicating that the **form, medium, and artistic style** of the figures can be changed. It further specifies changes to materials: “gilded brocade” for Mary’s robe, “richly draped velvet” for Joseph’s cloak, and “ornate silver swaddling” for the infant. These describe material transformations, not structural or symbolic alterations. The instruction also mandates placing them in a “detailed architectural setting with classical columns and divine motifs,” meaning the **background environment** must be changed from plain white to a complex frescoed mural.

However, the instruction does **not** mention changing the **identity**, **posture**, **relative positions**, or **core symbolic elements** (such as halos, lily staff, infant’s halo wings, or kneeling/standing poses). Therefore, these elements are implicitly required to remain recognizable despite stylistic transformation.

Allowed Changes List:  
- Material of clothing (e.g., fabric type: brocade, velvet, silver swaddling)  
- Artistic medium (from figurine to fresco)  
- Background setting (from plain white to detailed architectural fresco with columns and divine motifs)  
- Color palette of garments (as long as they align with new materials like gilded or silver)  
- Texture and rendering style (to match Renaissance fresco technique)

**Step 2 – Determine Elements That Should Remain Unchanged**

Thinking process:  
Core recognition elements must be preserved because they define the identities of the figures. For example:
- The **golden halo** around each figure is a universal Christian iconographic symbol identifying sanctity. Its presence is essential for recognizing Mary, Joseph, and Jesus.
- The **staff with a white lily** held by Joseph is a traditional attribute symbolizing purity and is uniquely associated with him in nativity scenes.
- The **infant Jesus’ golden radiant halo** (wing-like rays) is a distinctive sign of divine nature and must remain even if stylized.
- The **kneeling posture of Mary** and **standing posture of Joseph** are key narrative gestures that convey their roles and emotional states—these spatial relationships are critical to the scene's meaning.
- The **positioning of the infant between the two adults**, centered and elevated slightly, maintains the sacred triangle composition typical of nativity depictions.

Functional/logical association elements:
- The **staff held by Joseph** must remain attached to him and oriented appropriately (upward with lily tip), as it serves both symbolic and compositional functions.
- The **rocky bases** beneath the figures may be reimagined in fresco style but should still support the figures in the same relative positions to maintain stability and narrative grounding.

Non-modified element deduction:
- Although fabrics and colors can change per instructions, **the shape and structure of garments** (e.g., Mary’s draped shawl, Joseph’s layered robes) must remain consistent enough to preserve character identity.
- The **infant’s pose** (arms crossed over chest, legs bent, lying on back) is a classic representation and should be retained for recognition.
- The **white fluffy surface under the infant** may be replaced by a frescoed version (e.g., painted clouds or straw), but its function as a soft resting place must be preserved visually.

Preservation Targets List:  
- Golden halo around Mary  
- Golden halo around Joseph  
- Golden halo (radiant wings) behind infant Jesus  
- Staff with white lily held by Joseph  
- Kneeling posture of Mary  
- Standing posture of Joseph leaning on rock  
- Infant Jesus lying on back with arms crossed  
- Relative positioning: Mary left, Joseph right, infant center  
- Rock base under Mary and Joseph  
- White fluffy surface under infant (functionally equivalent)
